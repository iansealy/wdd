Usability heuristics

What is a heuristic?

Nielsen's classification of usability heuristics

Nielsen's heuristics provide 10 general usability principals for ANY interactive system. (not just websites!)

1. Visibility of system status

  • Inform the user what the system is doing (e.g. navigation, confirmation of a purchase, changes in state)

Examples: Warning messages ("are you sure?"), status bars for site loading ("percent downloaded" better than "Loading...")

For example, www.expedia.co.uk and http://www.play.com

2. Match between system and real world

  • Speak the user's language
  • Don't use non-standard meanings
  • View interactions from user's perspective (e.g. "you have bought...", rather than "we have sold you...")
  • Make use of mental models (based on task analysis) or use appropriate metaphors (such as "shopping cart")

3. User control and freedom

  • Users don't like to feel trapped, e.g. in a sequence of steps (registration or shopping cart)
  • Don't force new windows on users; let them choose! Discussion!
  • Also, good navigation (Where can I go? Always provide a link to home page in case they get lost)

4. Consistency & standards

  • Basic usability principle
  • Same info should be presented in same location on all pages & formatted in the same way. ocean letting example
  • This includes functionality as well as screen design, e.g. shopping baskets ought to all work in a similar manner

5. Help users recognise, diagnose and recover from errors

  • Should be phrased in clear language and avoid obscure codes (e.g. the notorious Error 404!)
  • Should be precise, e.g. instead of "cannot open this document", "Cannot open Chapter 5.doc because this file does not exist" might be more informative
  • Should be constructive, e.g. add "search your computer for this file" to the above example
  • Should be polite and not intimidate the user, e.g. "ILLEGAL USER ACTION, JOB ABORTED!"

6. Error prevention

  • Even better than having good error messages is preventing them in the first place
  • Avoid errors by doing lots of testing!

This is Defensive Design.

7. Recognition rather than recall

  • Easier to recognise information than remember it (e.g. when learning a language, passive vocabulary is much larger than active vocabulary)
  • Get user to choose between clear options, e.g. entering date in a form
  • Use generic commands (e.g. File>Open, common labelling schemes such as "about us", "contact"), which promote cross-site / cross-application learning

8. Flexibility and ease of use

  • "Accelerators" make it easy for users to carry out certain tasks, e.g. function keys, contextual menus, bookmarks, "Remember Me" login options
  • Shortcuts also important in accessibility - can be added using "accesskey" attribute

Examples of accelerators include "Ctrl-C" (copy) in Windows and Amazon's "1-click ordering".

9. Aesthetic and Minimalist Design

  • Simplify as much as possible
  • Understand users' mental models
  • Make navigation simple
  • Follow "Gestalt" principles such as closure, similarity and proximity
  • Avoid extraneous information

10. Help and documentation

  • Ideal system has no need for help, as it is 100% intuitive!
  • Unfortunately, most users don't read help
  • When they do, though, provide a good, task-oriented search and integrate documentation within your site
  • Also, put links to help where users will look for them, preferably in the "locus of attention"; this is contextual help.

  • Bad usability is easy to notice. Resist the temptation to blame users rather than the system!

Heuristic evalution

The goal is to find the usability problems in a user interface design. This is best done by several experts, but general approach can be used by anyone to assess the usability of a site. This method is also known as an expert evaluation or a usability review.

See also: Heuristic Evaluation - a Step By Step Guide, How to Conduct a Heuristic Evaluation

Steps

  1. Determine what you are going to evaluate (e.g. paper prototype, HTML prototype, existing website)
  2. Select the evaluators. Ideally, 3 or more usability experts (the more experienced the better)
  3. Do the evaluation
  4. Compile results & deliver summary report

The evaluation

  • Look for areas of confusion or where they feel users might potentially be confused
  • Each assessor should assess the site in isolation
  • Can be useful to go through the site in two ways (i) a general browse, (ii) task-specific
  • Each problem should also be assigned a severity rating in order to prioritise usability problems
    • Severity 1: Cosmetic problem - need not be fixed unless extra time is available on the project
    • Severity 2: Minor problem - should be fixed, but low priority
    • Severity 3: Major problem - important to fix and should be given high priority
    • Severity 4: Usability catastrophe - imperative to fix this!

Compiling the results

  • Sort each problem by a severity rating
  • Classify problems according to Nielsen's heuristics
  • Discuss results with other evaluators
  • Deliver a summary report. Be positive and constructive! Phrase the report in terms of design improvements rather than being overly critical

A sample heuristic evaluation

Limitations of heuristic evaluation

  • Only one of many techniques
  • Ideally requires several experts
  • Not particularly systematic (although individual evaluators may have a personal systematic approach)
  • Doesn't involve real users; still a second-guess, although based on well-known and well-established usability heuristics

Task

You play the role of a usability expert. Carry out a heuristic analysis of the psychometrics centre

Tips

  • Base your analysis on existing usability checklists, such as those at Bunnyfoot and Usability.gov (NOTE that these are by no means exhaustive)
  • Try a 2-step approach: 1. get an overall feel for the site. 2. put yourself in the mind of a potential user and try to carry out what you consider to be important tasks for the site.
  • Try to classify your usability findings into meaningful groups. e.g. you could try putting them into Nielsen's categories, or the site may suggest a different grouping to you.
  • AFTER you have done this, get together with one of your esteemed usability colleagues(!) to discuss your findings. How does your evaluation differ from theirs?
