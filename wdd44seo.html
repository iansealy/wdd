Search Engine Optimisation

Kieren Pitts

The Web is huge

  • Over 1 billion sites in May 2016 according to Netcraft
  • Yahoo! and Google stopped listing the number of pages they index in August 2005
  • But WorldWideWebSize.com estimates 3.99 billion (indexed) pages were indexed on 16th June 2016
  • In 2013, Google announced they'd found over 30 thousand trillion URLs

Directories and Search engines

  • Directories:
    • Are human-generated
    • Have descriptions
    • Have a classification system
    • For example, Open Directory Project
    • Yahoo! closed its main directory in December 2014
  • Search engines:
    • Are computer-generated indexes
    • Created by a spider crawling web sites (or other search engines?)
    • For example, Google, Bing and Yahoo!
  • Therefore search engines should be comprehensive, but directories should be of higher quality

More Directories and Search engines

  • Was clear split between directories and search engines, but not now:
    • Yahoo! started as a directory, then created a search and closed the directory
    • Google started as a search engine but used Open Directory Project, branded as Google Directory, for 11 years but closed it in July 2011
  • Users don't care about directories and many companies are closing them but it can be important to get new sites listed in directories
  • Google trusts some directories to a certain degree and uses content from them because they are maintained by humans
  • A good way to increase your site's reputation

Search engine submission - ODP/DMOZ

  • Provides a directory used by many sites
  • Submission is free
  • Site reviewed by a human editor
  • Resubmit every 6 weeks if not listed
  • Submission details

Search engine submission - Google

  • Can submit to Google for free
  • May take a long time to be listed
  • Google AdWords appear as Sponsored Links
    • Pay per click
    • Set a maximum CPC (cost per click) and a daily budget
  • (AdSense - contextual text ads from Google displayed on your site)

How Search Engines Rank Pages

  • Originally ranking algorithms were very simple:
    • Count number of times the search term appears on a page and rank pages with the highest number of occurrences more highly
    • Give higher ranking if search term appears in title or near start of page
    • Give higher ranking if search term appears in metadata
  • These simple algorithms were open to abuse by filling your page with lots of unrelated terms that are frequently searched for (e.g. viagra or david beckham)
  • Ranking algorithms now mainly use reliable "off-the-page" metrics, for example, give a higher ranking to sites that are heavily linked to from other sites (since the best sites will be linked to more often)
  • Relies on the fact that the text of a hyperlink to another site is likely to be highly related to the content of the page being linked to

Building traffic

  • You can't get lots of traffic just by adding some metadata and submitting to search engines
  • Getting listed by directories helps, but directories only list quality sites, so you need to make sure the site is good
  • To get a high ranking in search engines, need to be linked to from many sites (which can be measured - although Google Search Console is better for your own site), but this will only happen if site is good (although there are exceptions, e.g. "awards" scam) and time/money is invested
  • Follow the quality guidelines set out by the search engines:
    • Google Webmaster Guidelines
    • Yahoo! Search Content Quality Guidelines
    • Bing Guidelines

Google ranking

  • (Google's algorithm is often called PageRank, but that's just one part of its algorithm)
  • PageRank is a numerical value that determines the relevance of a Web page to a search term
  • Assessment of the number and types of links made to the page

More Google ranking

  • PageRank is a logarithmic scale
  • Link quality is as important as link quantity (more important to have one link from a reputable, relevant site than 10 links from unrelated sites)
  • The context of each link is assessed (location on page; whether surrounded by relevant text) and so is the ownership (links from domains with the same ownership or the same IP address are less valuable)
  • Google also examines page structure
  • PageRank determines how deeply your site is crawled by Googlebot

Search Engine Optimisation

  • Aim of search engine optimisation (SEO) is for your page to appear high up in the results listings for a given phrase
  • Pick target phrases
  • Don't choose single words as competition is too great - time and money could be better spent (e.g. on Web-based advertising or conventional advertising)
  • These phrases must be decided on early as they are also how you'll measure success
  • Each page will have different target keywords
  • Keywords for one page might be "English counties", but for another might be "Surrey history"
  • Do a search using the phrases you have chosen and record where your site is ranked
  • You may need to go through many results pages to find your site (if at all)

Keyword Placement

  • Position your target keywords on your page
  • Phrase should appear in title (but title is an exceptionally important piece of information and must still be readable by humans) and, ideally, URL
  • Phrase should appear near top of page (in headings and text)
  • Table-based design may push content further down page; using CSS-P means the content can come first
  • Build links to your pages (both external and internal)
  • Depending on sector, contact sites that are top for your target phrase and request a link

Titles Aside

  • Title should be unique
  • Important terms should be at the start of the title (so your site's name should be last)
  • Google only displays first 66 characters of title, so key terms need to be before that
  • (Meta tags aside)

More Keyword Placement

  • Not all links are equal - links from a relevant site count more
  • Try to ensure links include target phrase
  • Ensure phrases aren't contracted (e.g. always refer to "English counties" and not just "counties")
  • Monitor rank in major search engines over time
  • Monitor your site using tools provided by the search engines, e.g. Google Search Console
  • Long scrolling pages pose some SEO problems because of key word positioning and also making it harder for people to link to your content and not using unique URLs
  • Be aware that rankings can go down as well as up
  • Relevancy can seem like magic - small changes can produce a big effect

SEO Dos and Don'ts

Don't...

  • Don't try to deceive search engines
  • Don't use link spamming
  • Don't use CSS or server-side tricks to hide content from users but make it visible to spiders (cloaking), e.g. hidden white words on a white background
  • Consequences can be severe

SEO Dos and Don'ts

Don't...

  • Don't join a "free for all" link exchange program but do exchange links with other well respected and relevant Web sites
  • Don't "optimise" your pages for spiders at the expense of usability/readability for human users - Googlebot can "spot" optimised pages
  • Don't rely on JavaScript
  • If you have any doubts that what you're doing isn't ethical, don't do it

SEO Dos and Don'ts

Do...

  • Do produce interoperable and accessible pages - think of Googlebot as a blind user
  • Do ensure your site has no broken links
  • Do update your site regularly with interesting and relevant content that others want to link to
  • Do use Google Sitemaps and Google Webmaster Tools
  • Do avoid using Flash, images, Word documents, PDFs, etc... for text-based content wherever possible

SEO Dos and Don'ts

Do...

  • Do avoid duplicating content (or the same content accessible from multiple URLs)
  • Do minimise number of domains (www.amazon.co.uk, not books.amazon.co.uk and dvds.amazon.co.uk)
  • Do publish RSS
  • Do make it easy for users to bookmark your pages
  • Do consider publishing linkbait
  • Do behave ethically and responsibly

Google Panda

  • Algorithm update first released in February 2011
  • Reduce the rank of low quality or 'thin' sites
  • Results in a site-wide lowering of traffic/rank
  • Some evidence that Panda unfairly affects small businesses possibly due to problems determining true authority
  • Criticism led to a softer panda
  • Guidance on building high quality sites

Google Penguin

  • Algorithm update first released in April 2012
  • Reduce the rank of content on sites that break Google's guidelines (specifically those engaging in 'back-hat' SEO)
  • Results in a lowering of traffic/rank for individual pages/sections
  • Penalties can be caused by:
    • Excessive/unnatural back links
    • Key word stuffing
    • Publishing content on other websites for the purposes of link building
    • Comment spam
  • What if you didn't create the back links? Google's disavow tool
  • Changes to Google's algorithm can be powerful enough to put companies out of business or cause redundancies

Google Mobilegeddon

  • "Mobile-friendliness as a ranking factor"
  • Announced February 2015
  • Searches from mobiles will feature mobile-friendly sites more prominently
  • Rolled out 21st April 2015
  • Test pages to see if they are mobile friendly
  • Mobile usability report within Google Search Console

Encrypt everywhere

  • "HTTPS as a ranking signal"
  • Announced August 2014
  • HTTPS everywhere benefits all users
  • Services such as Let's Encrypt provide SSL certificates for free
  • Let's Encrypt certificates supported by Dreamhost
  • Firefox proposes the deprecation of HTTP
  • Chromium and Chrome plan to warn users if a site still uses HTTP
  • For e-commerce worth considering certificates with insurance and more checks of ownership

Advanced Searching Techniques

Phrase searching

  • Search for test cricket and "test cricket" on Google

Inclusions and exclusions

  • Prefixing with + or - can include or exclude specific terms
  • Search for "kieren pitts" -ILRT on Google

Advanced Searching Techniques

Boolean searching

  • Boolean operators allow you to define specific relationships between elements of your search term
  • The most common operators are AND, OR and NOT (which on some search engines are abbreviated to &, | and ! respectively)
  • Search for "ian sealy" | "kieren pitts" on Google)

Advanced Searching Techniques

Wildcards

  • Wildcards are useful in ensuring that related terms are not inadvertently overlooked
  • Searching for seal* will match seal, Sealy, seals, sealed, sealing, etc...
  • Searching for ~seal on Google will search for synonyms of seal

Google Advanced Operators

Google has some advanced operators that allow you to be even more specific with your searching, for example:

  • define - look up a definition for a term e.g. define:entomology
  • site - limit your search to pages from a particular site e.g. kieren site:www.bris.ac.uk
  • link - find pages that link to your site e.g. link:www.bris.ac.uk
