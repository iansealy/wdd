Huge generalisation but let's assume 300 pages per site.

So there are: 51,600,000,000 pages

Yahoo and Google stopped listing the number of pages they search in August 2005.

Directories and Search engines

  • Directories:
    • Are human-generated
    • Have descriptions
    • Have a classification system
    • For example, Yahoo! and the Open Directory Project
  • Search engines:
    • Are computer-generated indexes
    • Are created by a robot or spider
    • For example, Google and Yahoo!

Therefore search engines should be comprehensive, but directories should be of higher quality.

Searching the Web

Use directories for popular or general terms, e.g. biology

Use search engines for esoteric terms, e.g. search for whale watching vancouver

Results returned from search engines often come from a variety of sources. The main results could come from one source, the paid advertising from another and directory listings from a third.

However, Yahoo! produces its own search results, paid for links and directory. Google has its own search results and adverts but uses the ODP for the directory.

Search engine submission - Yahoo! Directory

Yahoo! Directory

  • Can submit non-commercial sites for free to non-commercial categories
  • May take a long time to be reviewed (if at all)
  • For commercial sites and commercial categories, must use Yahoo! Directory Submit
  • For site to be reviewed the (non-refundable) cost is $299 ($600 if site contains adult content)
  • Not a guarantee of a listing, but reviewed within 7 days
  • If accepted, annual renewal fee $299 ($600 for adult content)

Search engine submission - Yahoo Search

  • Free submission - not guaranteed and can take a long time
  • Paid submission
    • Basic = $49 per URL, up to 5 URLs per domain
    • Pro = cost based on clickthroughs and category of content
    • Guaranteed inclusion (assuming you renew)
    • URLs crawled every 48 hours
    • Submission into Yahoo!, AltaVista, AllTheWeb.

Search engine submission - ODP

  • Open Directory Project:
    • Provides directory for Google, AOL Search and others
    • Submission is free
    • Site reviewed by a human
    • Resubmit every 6 weeks if not listed

Search engine submission - Google

Google

  • Can submit to Google for free
  • May take a long time to be listed
  • Google AdWords appear as Sponsored Links
    • Pay per click
    • Set a maximum CPC (cost per click) and a daily budget
    • Premium service costs minimum of $10,000 per month!
  • AdSense - contextual text ads from Google displayed on your site. Can bid for terms.

How Search Engines Rank Pages

  • Ranking algorithms used to be very simple:
    • Count number of times the search term appears on a page and rank pages with the highest number of occurrences more highly
    • Give higher ranking if search term appears in title or near start of page
    • Give higher ranking if search term appears in metadata
  • These simple algorithms are open to abuse by filling your page with lots of unrelated terms that are frequently searched for (e.g. "Paris Hilton" etc.)
  • Ranking algorithms now also use reliable "off-the-page" metrics, for example, give a higher ranking to sites that are heavily linked to from other sites (since the best sites will be linked to more often)
  • Relies on the fact that the text of a hyperlink to another site is likely to be highly related to the content of the page being linked to

Building traffic

  • You can't get lots of traffic just by adding some metadata and submitting to search engines
  • Getting listed by directories helps, but directories only list quality sites, so you need to make sure the site is good
  • To get a high ranking in search engines, need to be linked to from many sites (which can be measured), but this will only happen if site is good (although there are exceptions, e.g. "awards" scam) and time/money is invested
  • Follow the quality guidelines set out by the search engines: Google Webmaster Guidelines and Yahoo! Search Content Quality Guidelines

Google PageRank review

  • PageRank is a numerical value that determines the relevance of a Web page to a search phrase
  • Assessment of the number and types of links made to the page
  • PageRank for a page is displayed if you're using the Google Toolbar
  • PageRank does not use meta tags
  • How old is your domain?
  • How long has a link to your site persisted?

Google PageRank review 2

  • Google also monitors the rate your site gains/loses links
  • Frequency of updates is monitored as are rapid changes in the number of keywords associated with a page
  • IP address may be monitored - issues for shared hosting
  • Admin and tech details may be checked for consistency
  • Further info on PageRank

Search engine submission - Google Sandbox

New sites are put 'on probation' after being first crawled and don't rank highly for popular search terms.

  • Combat spam sites using many paid for links on day of launch or repeat offenders
  • New sites will rank well for less common keywords
  • One to six months in the Sandbox (based on keyword)
  • There are no shortcuts, you must wait until your site comes out of the Sandbox 'naturally'
  • Slow progress after leaving sandbox

There are also other dampening filters.

Search engine optimisation

  • Aim of search engine optimisation (SEO) is for your page to appear high up in the results listings for a given phrase
  • Pick target phrases
  • Don't choose single words as competition is too great - time and money would be better spent (on Web-based advertising or conventional advertising)
  • These phrases must be decided on early as they are also how you'll measure success
  • Each page will have different target keywords
  • Keywords/phrases for one page might be "English counties", but for another might be "Surrey history"

Search engine optimisation 2

  • Do a search using the phrases you have chosen and record where your site is ranked
  • You may need to go through many pages of listings to find your site
  • You can also use the Google Toolbar to list your page ranking

Key word placement

  • Position your target keywords on your page
  • Phrase should appear in title (but title is an exceptionally important piece of information and must still be readable to humans)
  • Phrase should appear near top of page (in headings and text)
  • Table-based design (nav bars, etc...) may push content further down page - using CSS-P means the content can come first
  • Build links to your pages (both external and internal)
  • Contact sites that are top for your target phrase and request a link

Key word placement 2

  • Not all links are equal - links from a relevant site count more
  • Try to ensure links include target phrase
  • Ensure phrases aren't contracted (e.g. always refer to "English counties" and not just "counties")
  • Monitor rank/position in major search engines over time - search for your chosen phrases and compare your current position in the ranking with that previously recorded
  • Monitor your site using tools provided by the search engines: Google Webmaster tools
  • Be aware that rankings can go down as well as up
  • Relevancy can seem like magic - small changes can produce a big effect

SEO Dos and Don'ts

Don't...

  • Don't try to deceive search engines....ever, ever, ever!
  • Don't use Link Spamming
  • Use CSS or server side tricks to hide content from users but make it visible to spiders. For example having hidden white words on a white background. You will be punished severely
    Further info on Hidden links
  • Don't join a "free for all" link exchange program but do exchange links with other well respected and relevant Web sites
  • Don't "optimise" your pages for spiders at the expense of usability/readability for human users. Googlebot can "spot" optimised pages
  • If you have any doubt about what you're doing as being ethical, don't do it.

SEO Dos and Don'ts

Do...

  • Do produce interoperable and accessible pages - think of Googlebot as a blind user
  • Do ensure your site has no broken links
  • Do update your site regularly with interesting and relevant content that others want to link to
  • Do use Google Sitemaps and Google Webmaster tools
  • Do avoid using Flash, images, Word documents, PDFs etc for text-based content wherever possible
  • Do behave ethically and responsibly

Advanced Searching Techniques

Phrase searching

Search for ian sealy and "ian sealy" on Live Search.

Inclusions and exclusions

Prefixing with + or - can include or exclude specific terms.

Search for "kieren pitts" -ILRT on Google.

Advanced Searching Techniques 2

Boolean searching

Boolean operators allow you to define specific relationships between elements of your search term.

The most common operators are AND, OR and NOT (which on some search engines can be abbreviated to &, | and ! respectively).

Search for "ian sealy" OR "kieren pitts" on Google).

NEAR

NEAR is an operator that specifies that a word appears close to (but not immediately adjacent to) another word.

Search for ian NEAR sealy on AltaVista.

Advanced Searching Techniques 3

Wildcards

Wildcards are useful in ensuring that related terms are not inadvertently overlooked.

Searching for seal* will match seal, Sealy, seals, sealed, sealing, etc...

Searching for ~seal on Google will search for synonyms of seal.

Restrictions

Searchs can be refined by restricting to certain attributes of a Web page.

Search for title:"Ian Sealy" or "Kieren Pitts" +host:bris.ac.uk on AltaVista.

Google Advanced Operators

Google has some advanced operators that allow you to be even more specfic with your searching, for example:

  • define - look up a definition for a term e.g. define:entomology
  • site - limit your search to pages from a particular site e.g. kieren site:www.bris.ac.uk
  • link - find pages that link to your site e.g. link:www.bized.co.uk

Further info: http://www.google.co.uk/intl/en/help/features.html
