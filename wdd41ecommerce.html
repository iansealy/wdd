eCommerce

eCommerce stands for electronic commerce

eCommerce covers more than online sales:

  • Advertising
  • Real-time stock levels
  • Creation of estimates
  • Auctions

However, we'll be focusing on online sales.

Value to the economy

Online sales are a significant proportion of the economy.

Crucially it makes international trade easier.

  • UK consumers spent over £91 billion online in 2013 - estimates for 2014 suggest more than £107 billion
  • Total annual growth in online shopping within the UK is around 12%
  • 73% of the UK population shop online

Online sales

Can be done in many ways but rely on some, or all, of:

  • Product catalogue
  • Shopping cart/basket
  • Collection of delivery information
  • Online payment
  • Order tracking

In many cases "Online payment" is handled by a third party.

Selling online

Many different ways to sell products or services online.

Can be broadly categorised as:

  1. Simple forms on your site
  2. Selling on someone else's site
  3. Hosted shops
  4. Running your own shop

Simple forms

Third parties provide code/instructions, for example:

  • PayPal Payments Standard

Pros and cons:

  • Easy to implement, just add their code to your site
  • Useful if you have few products
  • Doesn't scale well
  • Not always easy/possible to customise
  • Some rely solely on JavaScript for shopping cart functionality

Example: Online donations

Selling on someone else's site - 1

Some existing sites allow you to:

  1. Compete with other merchants for orders
  2. Add products to their catalogue

For example:

  • Sell on Amazon
  • Amazon Marketplace
  • CafePress

Selling on someone else's site - 2

Pros and cons:

  • Shop hosted/maintained by someone else
  • Orders sometimes fulfilled by third party
  • Not practical if you have niche products
  • Benefit by association
  • Often little or no control of look and feel
  • Hard to integrate with an existing web site
  • Others take a cut of profits

Example: 8PawsUp

Hosted shops

Shop software written and hosted by a third party. For example:

  • Amazon Webstore
  • eBay stores
  • Shopify
  • 3Dcart

Pros and cons:

  • Shop hosted/maintained by someone else
  • Admin area for you to run your shop
  • Often have some control over look and feel by picking themes
  • Can be hard to integrate with an existing web site

Example: Foo Fighters (Shopify)

Hosting your own shop

Host a shop using software written by yourself or a third party. For example:

  • Zen Cart
  • Magento
  • Actinic
  • OpenCart

Pros and cons:

  • Often have complete flexibility over look and feel
  • Some software allows you to edit core code to change shop functionality
  • Hosting and upgrades can be tricky/expensive
  • Easy to integrate with an existing web site

Example: AES shop

Accepting online payments

If hosting your own shop or using a hosted shop you'll need a way for people to pay you.

There are three ways to take online payments

  1. Collecting and processing credit card details yourself
    • Often only practical for large companies
    • Payment Card Industry (PCI) Data Security Standards are difficult for small business to achieve
    • Is it worth the risk?
  2. Using a Merchant account
  3. Using a Payment gateway

Merchant account

Using a "Merchant account" requires two things:

  1. Acquiring bank - a bank (usually a high street bank) that will process credit card sales
  2. Internet Merchant Service - a service that allows you to accept cards online by using a Payment Service Provider (PSP) that passes the details to the acquiring bank

Example: RBS WorldPay Business Gateway Plus

  • Provides both a Merchant account with an Acquiring bank and an Internet Merchant Service
  • Setup fee = £75
  • Monthly fee = £19.95 per month
  • Transaction charge = 1.65% + 10p per transaction

Merchant account - pros and cons

Pros and cons of a Merchant account:

  • If you already process card payments (e.g. you run a physical shop) it can be quick to set up
  • Payment is often via a "well known" bank
  • Starting from scratch can require credit checks and take up to two months for approval
  • May be refused if you have no trading history
  • You usually need to pay for the Payment Service Provider
  • Acquiring Bank also charge a percentage of each transaction
  • Banks may want to see a business plan or other documentation
  • Some business types may be rejected:-
    • Gambling
    • Selling replica products
    • Medical supplies/pharmacy
    • Selling "adult" products

Payment gateways - 1

Payment gateways use their own acquiring services so they act as the Acquiring bank and provide the Internet Merchant Service.

Payment Gateways make money from lots and lots of small transactions.

Pros and cons:

  • Easy to set up
  • Often well known and customers may have an account already
  • No credit checks - authorisation commonly by way of associated credit card account
  • No need for a business plan
  • Funds often held for the first 30-60 days after an account opened as fraud prevention measure

Payment gateways - 2

Pros and cons continued:

  • Easy to integrate with an online shop
  • Easy to issue refunds
  • Charges are low:
    • PayPal - 1.4%-3.4% + £0.20 per transaction
    • Sage Pay - £25 a month or 10p per transaction depending on number of transactions
    • Braintree Payments - 2.4%-3.4% + £0.20 per transaction

Security issues 1

Security issues to consider when running online shops.

  • Data sent over Secure Sockets Layer (SSL)
    • SSL encrypts the data sent back and forth between server and browser so it can't be read en-route
    • SSL should be used whenever personal data is requested - not just when collecting payment details
    • SSL encryption should also be used in the admin area of your shop
    • SSL certificates, and the additional server setup required, usually adds to your hosting costs. SSL certificates can cost more than hosting (£50 - £200+)
    • An aside - never send credit card details by email
  • Access control
    • Only those that need access to the admin areas of the shop should have access
    • Choose strong passwords
    • Don't re-use passwords!

Security issues 2

  • Third party shop software
    • Choosing software
      • Read reviews
      • Check rate of releases
      • How active is the community?
      • Check Secunia for unpatched security issues
    • Review the initial setup
    • Remove any shop components you don't need
    • Subscribe to relevant mailing lists so you are informed of:
      • Updates and patches to software
      • New versions of software
      • Bug reports

Security issues 3

  • Backups
    • Make sure you have backups of the data
      • Automate
      • Choose a sensible frequency
      • Keep sequential backups
      • Don't keep backups in the same place as the original data
      • Not just good practice for online shops - your project work?
    • Don't rely on your host for backups... ever... ever... ever
    • Encrypt backups

Legal issues overview

There are a variety of laws covering online sales, including:-

  • Consumer Protection (Distance Selling) Regulations 2000 and amendments 2005
  • Electronic Commerce (EC Directive) Regulations 2002
  • Data Protection Act 1998
  • Sale of Goods Act 1979 - ensures seller has the right to sell, the product is as expected, product of satisfactory quality
  • Equality Act 2010 - accessibility
  • Privacy and Electronic Communications (EC Directive) Regulations 2003 (and amendments in 2004 and 2011) - covers email marketing, cookies and spam

An overview of the first three are provided on the following slides (plus a little about Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations). However, I'm not a lawyer, so you should familiarise yourself with the legal issues associated with running an eCommerce site. A good place to start is Out-Law.com

Legal issues - Distance Selling Regulations

Provides consumers the right to:-

  • Prior to purchase, information about:
    • the supplier
    • the goods/service being purchased
    • the process of the sale
    • delivery costs
  • notification of the 7 day cancellation period
  • what happens in the event of goods being unavailable
  • confirmation of the above in writing (email not a web page)
  • protection from card fraud

Legal issues - Ecommerce regulations 1

Ecommerce sites must provide, in an easily accessible location, the following information :-

  • the name of the service provider
  • an email address of the provider - a contact form is insufficient
  • a postal address for the supplier
  • clear details of pricing, including postage and packing
  • company/charity registration number and membership number for any professional bodies
  • VAT number, if business is VAT registered
    • Must display VAT number anyway

Legal issues - Ecommerce regulations 2

The regulations also state:

  • must provide means to correct errors in order before the order placed
  • confirmation of order must be provided ASAP after order placed
  • provide clear terms and conditions including how/when the contract is formed
    • Critical for protection against pricing errors

Legal issues - Data Protection

Anyone who processes personal information must comply with eight principles of the Data Protection Act, which make sure that personal information is:

  1. Fairly and lawfully processed
  2. Processed for limited purposes
  3. Adequate, relevant and not excessive
  4. Accurate and up to date
  5. Not kept for longer than is necessary
  6. Processed in line with your rights
  7. Secure
  8. Not transferred to other countries without adequate protection

Source: Office of the Information Commissioner – http://www.ico.gov.uk

Legal issues - Data Protection - transferring data

One of the eight principles ensures that personal information is:

  • not transferred to other countries without adequate protection

Almost all countries outside of the European Economic Area (EEA) do not have adequate protection.

Do you know where your data is?

  • Where is your site hosted?
  • Where does the host back up that data to?

Companies within the US may sign up to the "Safe Harbor" scheme or you may take out an individual contract with any supplier (irrespective of the country they reside in) to ensure analogous levels of protection.

See also: Safe Harbor certification may not mean data protection compliance

Legal issues - Privacy and Electronic Communications Regulations

Covers email marketing etc, however...

2011 amendments to the regulations state that cookies or similar devices must not be used unless the subscriber or user:

  • is provided with clear and comprehensive information about the purposes of the storage of, or access to, that information; and
  • is given the opportunity to refuse the storage of, or access to, that information.

The only exceptions are cookies that are "strictly necessary" for provision of a service (e.g. shopping carts).

The regulations were described as "breathtakingly stupid" but came in to force on 26th May 2011 (the ICO gave organisations 12 months to comply).

Law has wider implications than for eCommerce sites but was changed at the last moment (May 2012) to include 'implied consent' .

However, even the ICO doesn't ask for consent any more. Although you should provide details of any cookies you set/use on your site.

Summary

A summary of things to consider:

  1. Consider your offline and online infrastructure and processes
  2. Should you run your own shop or a hosted solution?
    • If you run your own shop, how will you handle updates?
  3. What's the most cost-effective way for you to accept payments online?
  4. Do you have backups and a disaster recovery plan?
  5. Review the legal frameworks
  6. Ensure your site provides enough information to consumers
