WDD34 : Introduction

Where does usability fit into the development lifecycle?

  • Everywhere!
  • Usability engineering is iterative - should be applied throughout lifecyle

Definition of usability

A usable interface (e.g. for a website) has multiple components:

  • Learnability - should be easy to learn
  • Efficiency - should be productive
  • Memorability - should be easy to remember so that user can return later and not have to re-learn
  • Errors - should have a low error rate
  • Satisfaction - should be pleasant to use

What are the benefits of usability?

  • The benefits of usability shouldn't be underestimated; in some user interfaces (e.g. flight cockpits) it can be the difference between life and death
  • On the web, it can mean huge benefits in terms of revenues, repeat visits, customer satisfaction, efficiency etc.

The ROI of usability : some examples

ROI = Return on Investment

  • "A financial services company had to scrap an application it had developed, when, shortly before implementation, developers doing a User Acceptance test found a fatal flaw in their assumptions about how data would be entered. By this time, it was too late to change the underlying structure, and the application never implemented." (Dray, 1995)
  • "After move.com completed the redesign of the home "search" and "contact an agent" features based on a UI consulting firm's recommendations, users ability to find a home increased from 62% to 98%, sales lead generation to real estate agents increased over 150%, and [move.com's] ability to sell advertising space on move.com improved significantly." (Vividence, 2001)
  • "IBM's Web presence has traditionally been made up of a difficult-to-navigate labyrinth of disparate subsites, but a redesign made it more cohesive and user-friendly. According to IBM, the massive redesign effort quickly paid dividends. The company said in the month after the February 1999 re-launch that traffic to the Shop IBM online store increased 120 percent, and sales went up 400 percent." (Battey, 1999)

The ROI of usability : some more examples

  • "More than 83 percent of Internet users are likely to leave a Web site if they feel they have to make too many clicks to find what they're looking for, according to Andersen's latest Internet survey." (Arthur Andersen, 2001)
  • "To build a model intranet, Bay Networks spent $3 million and two years studying the different ways people think about the same thing. The result: all think alike about the $10 million saved each year." (Fabris, 1999)
  • "A study from Zona Research found that 62% of Web shoppers have given up looking for the item they wanted to buy online (and 20% had given up more than three times during a two-month period)." (Nielsen, October 1998)

    Summary of usability benefits

    Usable sites have:

    • decreased learning time
    • increased efficiency
    • decreased error rates
    • increased user enjoyment and trust e.g Stanford Web Credibility

    This leads to:

    • increased competitive advantage
    • improved customer satisfaction
    • improved brand image
    • improved user productivity
