Organising Information

Organisational challenges

Organizing complex information on a web site presents huge challenges:

  • Findability. Users need to be able to find what they want among a potentially huge numbers of items (e.g. taxfreegold.co.uk)
  • Ambiguity. Language is ambiguous, e.g. multiple definitions, cultural differences (the words "pitch", "catch")
  • Heterogeneity. "Objects composed of unrelated or unlike parts" Most Web sites are very heterogenous because they have multiple formats, usually all mixed up together
  • Differences in user perspectives. Ignoring different user perspectives can make parts of your site unusable; make sure that you know your user!

Organising information

You may not realize it, but we're all becoming librarians!

Information can be organized in the following ways:

  • Alphabetical, e.g. Cambridge Uni
  • Chronological, e.g. The food timeline
  • Geographical, e.g. Twittervision
  • Topical, e.g. DMOZ
  • Task-oriented, e.g. Blogger
  • Audience-specific, e.g UWE
  • Metaphor-driven, e.g. JK Rowling, Hothorse's old site.

Organisation structures: classification and hierarchies

Taxonomy is the classification of things.

  • e.g. Dewey Decimal System, Linnaean classification
  • Not all taxonomies are hierarchical e.g. days of week

Classification schemes provide important metadata for a Web site. They provide the basis for (i) efficient search and information retrieval and (ii) sharing of data between Web sites.

The hierarchy: a top-down approach

  • When designing hierarchies, usually have mutually exclusive categories
  • Balance of breadth versus depth, e.g. Amazon.co.uk and Amazon.com
  • Often stated that you should try to have "7 plus or minus 2" primary navigation options - this is a myth!
  • For sites expected to grow, it's easier to incorporate change into a broad and shallow design
  • Don't feel trapped by hierarchies - database or hypertexted approaches can sometimes be very useful

Relational databases: a bottom-up approach

  • Good way to manage information and use for multiple purposes
  • Content created "on-the-fly" depending on requirements, e.g. Amazon
  • This approach is technically more difficult (need understanding of SQL and a scripting language such as PHP, Perl, or ASP)

Metadata

Metadata is "Information about information"

Organizing data requires an understanding of metadata. Its the basis of all organization systems. There are three main types of metadata:

  1. Descriptive. The nature of the thing (subject, author etc) - the stuff that we're usually interested in classifying for our website
  2. Intrinsic. Properties of the medium (Document type, size etc)
  3. Administrative. Status of the item (edited? approved? temporary?)

An example of a widely known, controlled metadata scheme: Dublin Core

Informal metadata: folksonomies

As the web becomes more social and interactive, it becomes harder to maintain formal taxonomies and structures.

Informal structures are becoming much more common as we head towards Web2.0 and there's a move towards getting users to start tagging their own content e.g.

  • Flickr
  • del.icio.us

Tags are often visualised as a tag cloud

See also: the Wikipedia entry for Folksonomy
