Labelling and Classification

Developing a site architecture relies on a good understanding of labelling and classification.

Labelling systems

  • Can't present ALL information at once, so need to use informative short cuts, i.e. labels
  • These need to communicate information effectively

Why labels are important

  • Users have short attention spans (avoid high "cognitive load" for your users)
  • Bad labels make bad impressions; they frustrate users
  • Self-centred labelling makes a bad impression (avoid business-speak & terminology)

Labelling systems need serious planning.

An unplanned labelling system:

  • Technology Interface Unit
  • Project QA
  • Business & Media Interaction
  • Digital Cyberculture Expression
  • Faculty Skunkworks
  • Internal Services Office
  • New Media Center

These assume that the user knows what you are talking about!

A planned system:

  • Arts & Humanities
  • Business & Economy
  • Computers & Internet
  • Education
  • Entertainment
  • Government
  • Health
  • Reference
  • Regional

These might also make us wonder... e.g. what resources are contained within these categories? We do know what subject areas are covered, though. It's also a common system. Users have seen it before so they only need to learn the system, not individual labels (familiarity breeds contentment!)

Common labels within navigation systems

  • Main / main page / home / home page
  • Search / find / browse / sitemap / index / table of contents
  • Contact / feedback / contact webmaster
  • Help / FAQ / frequently asked questions
  • News / what's new
  • About / about us / about <company name> / who we are

Using metaphors

Real-world metaphors can be used as labels to support user understanding and goals. In fact, metaphors are everywhere:

  • Information Superhighway
  • Desktop
  • Trashcan
  • My Folder
  • Shopping Cart
  • Chat Room

But be careful with metaphors

Use metaphors wisely. Good metaphors make use of existing mental models (e.g. Shopping Cart), bad metaphors are contrived

Sometimes, metaphors that don't work too well in some contexts may work well in others; for example, geographical metaphors seem to work particularly well for children (e.g. Earth Science Explorer).

See also: Use and misuse of metaphor [PDF]

Card sorting

Card sorting is a simple, quick method for understanding how site users classify content (by shuffling cards around, hence the name). The method is used to generate an overall structure for your information, as well as suggestions for navigation, menus, and possible taxonomies.

Steps in a card sort

  1. Select the content to be tested (pages of existing site? new content?)
  2. Find the participants (should be representative of site users)
  3. Prepare the cards (write names of pages on cards)
  4. Conduct the tests
    • Open sort - participants create and label groups for the cards as they see fit
    • Closed sort - how do the cards fit into an existing classification? (validation of an existing classification)
  5. Analyse the results (common groupings? cluster analysis?)

Task

Try to group the content in a meaningful way (what is 'meaningful'?) and try to produce appropriate labels for the groups.
